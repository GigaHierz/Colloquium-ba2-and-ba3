Questions and concerns about our

community and its institutions

Colloquium BA2 & BA3

by PaulA Hierzi, Maria Ladopoulos, Judith Sanchez Ruiz, and Nina Wesemann

1. What does a community offer and what does it ask for?

The term community stems from ‘common’, already signalizing, that a community is built on something that is shared among a group of people; may it be interests, beliefs, goals, background, experience, etc. In a large group, like the dance community in Berlin, the uniting factors are often hard to find, making the trial to enter the community an opportunity for traumatic or isolated experiences.

When a community is established, however, it can create a sense of belonging for the people inside. The more the members of the community have in common the stronger, safer, and more empowering it gets. Therefore it is good to have smaller communities to communicate certain things, needs, experiences, and ideas. Then you will be able to open up to the bigger community. It should be possible to fluctuate between those communities according to your needs, and for the big community then to listen to their sub-groups (smaller communities) to be able to support and further the information with their power politically, within bigger institutions, and the outside.

Communities can be a place where an exchange of ideas, resources and information is usually accessible to not only longstanding members of the community but also new people coming from different cities or professions. Ideally then, the wide range of know-how, resources, funding opportunities, publicity, background, and financial support by the government, creates a breeding ground for shared learning, support and ideally even rest. People with larger public platforms can use that resource to support new or young artists. We can look for and find collaborators within the community, rather than reaching out to other cities.

Continuation is one of the essentials in a community (continuous projects, meetings, events, etc.). The community needs to have a shared vision, or as previously mentioned, some uniting factor that people feel nourished by. These commonalities lay the ground for mutual support, empowerment and accountability. These agreed-on factors will have to also be flexible, breathable, and well communicated to, one, not create a toxic environment through generalization, and, two, prevent further marginalization and tokenization of parts of the community.

2. How does the community correspond with the people outside?

We are asking this question in reflection to people who arrive as new members of the community, new visitors of performances and classes, and neighbors who surround the institutions.

New members of our dance community could be invited by experienced performers, dramaturges in a tandem way (for example: https://www.tanzraumberlin.de/tanzbuero/tandem/).

Former arrivers who are now integrated in the community will have the lived experience, knowledge of what makes it easier to arrive. Another way of introducing yourself in the dance scene can be through platforms, websites, festivals that give space for presentation, support, workshops.

Visitors of Performances who don’t have the knowledge or people to join the events can get to know each other or other interested visitors in small pre workshops before a performance, discussion rounds. Guides or an introduction before a performance can help to make visitors feel safer. (Tanzbüro is trying this on this platform: https://www.tanzraumberlin.de/tanzbuero/mapping-dance-berlin/)

To introduce neighbors into your space is an even more difficult question because they do not necessarily have an interest or a need for what you offer, it may even disturb them in the way that some institutions are also part of gentrification processes.

I personally think that it needs an openness to listen to neighbors and their needs and interests and to include them in an honest interaction. Otherwise, it will not be a safe space for the neighbors/participants to enter and participate in a respectful and mutual way.

Uferstudios has tried different things in their festival “Ausufern” (https://www.uferstudios.com/projekte/ausufern/) to get in contact with the Wedding that is surrounding them.

4. Is the community encouraging and supporting diversity within it?

“I have a hard time accepting diversity as a synonym for justice. Diversity is a corporate strategy. It’s a strategy designed to ensure that the institution functions in the same way that it functioned before, except now that you now have some black faces and brown faces. It’s a difference that doesn’t make a difference,” Angela Davis

A dance community is moving in a gray zone. There are institutions that see themselves as part of the community, where diversity needs to be implemented and enforced through structural changes. Especially the regularly state-funded institutions within the community are built on archaic foundations that are a breeding ground for exclusion, toxicity, and tokenization. Diversity does not end with having international dancers. Who are the leaders of these institutions? Which dance form is getting the regular funding? Who is profiting ? What structures in education and work are seen as successful and worth funding?

A large part of the dance community, though, is not routinely part of these institutions, which is where self-accountability and holding each other accountable becomes a major part of the work within the community.

In what I would call a mainstream contemporary dance technique education in Europe, you will learn to copy as many styles as possible in perfection to survive and be versatile on the market. With this, the acknowledgement and appreciation of the history of dance styles, especially in Non-Western cultural-heritage, gets often deleted and disrespected.

Of course, these approaches go hand in hand and will influence each other, but part of being in a community is listening to people with entirely different (lived) experiences and being open to the uncomfortable conversations that can come to the surface. As in any community, privileges play a huge part in our experiences, and if there is no open dialogue about these topics, there is no true community, but just further separation from one another.

5. How to deconstruct hierarchies in dance styles?

Dance-Styles as we call it here come with their intersectional history of culture, race, class, gender and sexuality. And those politics don’t just disappear when we talk about art, they are very present and affect funding and education.

They affect where, how and when you can earn a living with dance and choreography. The most important step is to acknowledge the influence and history and stop putting again and again only people up on the highest positions who come from a western, heteronormative dance background like ballet.

6. Why is there a lack of confidence in the work students and artists do in the network?

Often, especially in art, support and solidarity come under the caveat of subjective liking and taste of what is produced or what the other is part of. This can result in neglecting parts of the community and further separation within it.

There is a lack of honest and altruistic initiations. Feedbacking and Criticism morphed into controversial, even taboo topics. In some cases, art is seen more and more as a business, rather than a healer or an integral part of the system that feeds the multicultural, progressive, alternative Berlin. When genuine respect and appreciation in the artform is missing (and is being replaced by appreciation of names and status), the confidence inside the community will be lacking as well.

7. How do we support confidence and empathy within each other inside the community?

In an ideal world, we could find a way to celebrate the achievements of people inside the community, regardless of opinions based on personal taste or what we see as ‘valuable’. When we decipher our own personal sense of the difference between judgment and constructive criticism and are clearly able to communicate that to fellow artists; when we listen to each other's needs when peers communicate their boundaries of criticism, would we be able to create a safe space for creation and growth.

Feeling supported through the presence of other members of the community and, if needed, getting constructive feedback (which is always a sign of engagement and professionalism, regardless of liking something or not), is a good start to build the confidence to continue and do the same for someone else.

If funding would be distributed in a way that would create platforms directed and organized by different levels inside the community, inclusive and proactive, rather than formal and exclusive and with a narrow comfort in the known artist only, or their friends, we could achieve proposing a variety of styles, qualities, and decentralized points of view in art. Educate the community and, at the same time, educate the ones that are in power in what the community is lacking or suffering from.

8. What is our role and responsibility as artists?

With all the crises we are confronted with as a world today, art is needed more than ever. Start being an artist every day.

In the frame of the institution, start learning how to gather yourself with authenticity and firmness. Lead with certainty, spontaneous joy, and plenitude and stop being judgmental towards others, understand it is a much bigger picture than the one you allocate yourself as an artist. Don't wait until graduation or until a company hires you to activate your colleagues and peers' sense of responsibility.

Feedback each other with generosity and tolerance towards differences. Feed your intellect and vision without degrading others’ intellect and vision. We can value different approaches or unique ways of understanding in all kinds of educational and professional settings, and learn about them without having to agree and adopt them for the rest of our practice. There is a level of maturity needed to handle other people's points of view, to listen to (actual) constructive criticism and not only react to it. In educational situations, part of the responsibility lies in the students to keep the environment safe and when individual resistances to the approaches occur to not let it influence the energy, learning environment and togetherness of the whole group. At the same time, the teachers need to confront the group with flexibility and openness toward the individual.

The way of teaching, learning or working with each other, is to acknowledge the past of the person who is learning from you, to give space to their way of doing their art and finding a way to grow without diminishing each other. To be open to take advice without putting yourself down or forgetting what you want to express, to have the ability to grow with critique and see mistakes or being lost, as a helpful tool to grow and to acknowledge your individual pass. To see art, performance, etc. as a way of expression that will connect and teach not only you, but all participants; an expression that has an importance and duty only art can have.

We are moving in a community where, because of its wide and subjective character, the experiences people make are often polar opposite. While people can experience a resistance toward using physicality and certain trained technical backgrounds, people also experience feeling excluded from the community because of a lack of training. Similar struggles are discovered in narrative approaches or didactic approaches to pieces. It all comes back to respecting one another and not jumping to destructive conclusions and judgements, because this will always create divisions within the group. Judgment is disturbing, categorization dividing us.
